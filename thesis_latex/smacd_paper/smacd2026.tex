\title{Fuzzy Logic-Based MPPT for PV Battery Charger using 3 Input Parameters}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{Mehmet Gök, Kemal Ozanoglu, and Günhan Dündar}
\IEEEauthorblockA{\small{Department of Electrical and Electronics Engineering, Bogazici University}}

\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a fuzzy logic controller for a PV battery charger buck converter with modeling considered in an ASIC-oriented way using three input parameters: change in PV voltage ($\Delta V_{pv}$), change in output current ($\Delta I_{out}$), and PV voltage itself ($V_{pv}$). The approach avoids complex multiplication operations inherent in traditional MPPT algorithms. Simulation results validate the effectiveness of the proposed method under dynamic environmental conditions.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Fuzzy Logic Control, Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT), Photovoltaic (PV) Systems, Multi-Input Fuzzy Inference, Battery Charging, MPPT Optimization
\end{IEEEkeywords}

The I-V and power characteristics of the PV panel under various environmental conditions are shown in Figure \ref{fig:pv_curves}. As can be observed from the curves, despite significant variations in solar irradiance and temperature, the voltage corresponding to the Maximum Power Point (MPP) is generally confined to a relatively specific and narrow range. However, the exact MPP constantly shifts, requiring an active tracking mechanism. To effectively track this optimal point, the controller dynamically adjusts the duty cycle of the converter, thereby modulating the equivalent impedance seen by the PV panel. For instance, when the controller detects that both the PV voltage and current are increasing, the duty cycle needs to be reduced to pull the operating point closer to the new MPP. Conversely, if the PV voltage is increasing while the current is decreasing, it indicates a shift away from the optimal point on the P-V curve, and the duty cycle should be increased to steer the system continuously toward the MPP. A detailed explanation of the inference mechanism will be shared in Section \ref{subsec:fuzzy_controller}.

\subsection{Fuzzy Logic Controller}
\label{subsec:fuzzy_controller}

The fuzzy logic controller operates in three phases: Sampling \& Fuzzification, Inference, and Defuzzification \& Duty Adjustment.
The controller takes the three input parameters ($\Delta V_{pv}$, $\Delta I_{out}$, and $V_{pv}$) and produces the duty cycle change ($\Delta D$) as output.
The diagram is shown in Figure \ref{fig:fuzzy_diagram}.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{./images/matlab/fuzzy_controller_block_diagram.png}
\caption{Fuzzy logic controller block diagram showing the three phases of operation.}
\label{fig:fuzzy_diagram}
\end{figure*}

\subsubsection{Sampling \& Fuzzification}
In this phase, the three input parameters are sampled at the defined sampling frequency ($f_{sample} = 20$ kHz) using a 10-bit ADC for analog-to-digital converter. The first two parameters ($\Delta V_{pv}$ and $\Delta I_{out}$) are converted to digital form and their differences with previous sampled values are computed. The third parameter ($V_{pv}$) is taken directly without computing differences. These digital codes are then given to the inference part.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\columnwidth]{./images/matlab/line.png}
\caption{Linear segment used for membership degree calculation.}
\label{fig:membership_line}
\end{figure}

As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:membership_line}, the degree of membership is determined by mapping the crisp digital input onto the corresponding linear segment of the membership function. The membership value, which ranges from 0 to 255, is extracted using linear interpolation between the defined vertices of the fuzzy set. To avoid complex division operations and maintain an ASIC-friendly architecture, the interpolation fraction is approximated by rounding the denominator from 2046 to 2048 ($2^{11}$). This simplifies the calculation into a highly efficient bitwise right-shift ($\gg$) and addition:

\begin{equation}
    b = \frac{(a + 1023) \times 256}{2046} \approx (a \gg 3) + 128
    \label{eq:membership_calc}
\end{equation}
where $a$ represents the crisp digital input and $b$ represents the resulting 8-bit membership value.

\subsubsection{Inference}
The inference phase continuously evaluates a set of IF-THEN rules based on the three linguistic input variables: $\Delta V_{pv}$, $\Delta I_{out}$, and $V_{pv}$, using the \texttt{min} operator for the logical AND operation to determine the firing strength of each rule. The absolute PV voltage ($V_{pv}$) acts as a primary safety and reference parameter, aggressively steering the system with large steps when operating far from the MPP voltage range (Table \ref{tab:fuzzy_rules_safety}). When the voltage is within the optimal range, the controller uses the variations in voltage and current to precisely adjust the duty cycle, as defined in Table \ref{tab:fuzzy_rules_optimal}.

\subsubsection{Defuzzification \& Duty Adjustment}
In this final phase, a Sugeno-type weighted average is used to defuzzify the output of the inference process into a crisp value, $\Delta D$, according to the following equation:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta D = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i \cdot c_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i}
    \label{eq:defuzzification}
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the total number of active rules, $w_i$ represents the firing strength of the $i$-th rule obtained using the \texttt{min} operator, and $c_i$ is the constant crisp output coefficient defined for that rule. To further enhance the ASIC-oriented architecture and avoid computationally expensive operations, hardware simplifications are applied. Since the weight values ($w_i$) range between 0 and 1, the multiplication ($w_i \cdot c_i$) can be approximated using a simple look-up table that dictates either a conditional 1-bit right shift or no shift. For the denominator, given there is a maximum of 7 active rules, the summation of weights is evaluated using another simple look-up table that converts the complex division into a highly efficient bitwise right-shift operation by 0, 1, 2, or 3 bits. The precision loss (roughing) introduced by these approximations in both the fuzzification and defuzzification stages is effectively compensated for by subsequently dividing the final fuzzy output by a large factor. While this division intentionally slows the transient response, it is a deliberate and acceptable trade-off for preventing oscillations around the MPP and achieving better steady-state stability. A bumpless transfer mechanism is chosen while integrating this duty cycle change to ensure smooth transitions and prevent abrupt adjustments in the converter's operation.

\section{Simulation and Results}
\label{sec:results}

The performance of the proposed fuzzy logic-based MPPT controller was validated through a comprehensive simulation conducted in MATLAB/Simulink, with the overall results depicted in Figure \ref{fig:matlab_sim_result}. The total simulation time was 0.6 seconds, designed to demonstrate the controller's performance during both steady-state and transient conditions. The converter efficiency, calculated as $P_{out}/P_{in}$, was maintained at approximately 95\% throughout the simulation. The PV efficiency, defined as $P_{in}/P_{vmax}$, is the primary metric for evaluating the MPPT algorithm's effectiveness.

The simulation is divided into three distinct phases, with the critical transient moments highlighted in detail in Figure \ref{fig:matlab_sim_zooms}.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initial Stabilization (0s - 0.2s):} For the first 0.2 seconds, the system operates with a fixed duty cycle. The fuzzy logic controller is inactive, allowing the converter to stabilize without active MPPT control, as seen in the first section of Figure \ref{fig:matlab_sim_result}.

    \item \textbf{Fuzzy Controller Activation (0.2s):} At 0.2 seconds, the fuzzy logic MPPT controller is enabled. Figure \ref{fig:sim_zoom_02} shows a zoomed-in view of this event. The controller rapidly adjusts the duty cycle to find the maximum power point, achieving over 99\% PV efficiency in just 13.4 milliseconds, which highlights its fast convergence speed.

    \item \textbf{Transient Response (0.4s):} At 0.4 seconds, a step change simulates a sudden decrease in solar irradiance. Figure \ref{fig:sim_zoom_04} details this transient. Despite the disturbance causing a momentary dip in PV efficiency to 90\%, the fuzzy controller responds swiftly, restoring the efficiency to over 99\% in approximately 1 millisecond.
\end{enumerate}

These results confirm the robustness and high performance of the fuzzy logic controller, showcasing its ability to quickly track the MPP and maintain high efficiency, even under rapidly changing conditions.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This paper presents an advanced, ASIC-oriented Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT) approach for PV battery chargers utilizing a three-input fuzzy logic system. By evaluating the simultaneous changes in PV voltage ($\Delta V_{pv}$), output current ($\Delta I_{out}$), and the absolute PV voltage reference ($V_{pv}$), the proposed controller achieves highly responsive and accurate tracking. A major contribution of this work is the complete elimination of complex hardware multipliers and dividers within the fuzzy inference and defuzzification stages. By strategically constraining scaling factors and utilizing simple look-up tables coupled with bitwise shift operations, the computational complexity is significantly reduced, making the design highly suitable for efficient, low-cost integrated circuit implementations. 

Simulation results validate the effectiveness of this hardware-optimized approach, demonstrating exceptional steady-state PV tracking efficiency (exceeding 99\%) and high buck converter power conversion efficiency (around 95\% in continuous conduction mode), alongside a rapid transient recovery in the millisecond range during dynamic irradiance changes. Future work will focus on the complete physical integrated circuit (IC) implementation and experimental hardware validation of the proposed system.

To promote reproducibility and encourage further research, the complete MATLAB/Simulink models and associated code developed for this study are publicly available at \url{https://github.com/mehmetgok1/MATLAB}.
