\title{Hardware-Efficient Three-Input Fuzzy MPPT for PV Battery Charger ICs}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{Mehmet Gök, Kemal Ozanoglu, and Günhan Dündar}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Maximizing power extraction from photovoltaic (PV) arrays under dynamically changing environmental conditions is a critical challenge in renewable energy systems. Specifically for PV battery charging applications, conventional Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT) algorithms often struggle with slow transient responses and steady-state oscillations. To address these limitations, this paper presents an ASIC-oriented fuzzy logic controller modeled in MATLAB/Simulink that utilizes three input parameters: change in PV voltage ($\Delta V_{pv}$), change in output current ($\Delta I_{out}$), and absolute PV voltage ($V_{pv}$). The proposed architecture is designed to eliminate complex hardware multipliers and dividers by proposing the use of simple look-up tables and bitwise shift operations, making it highly suitable for low-cost integrated circuit implementations. 
Simulation results demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method, achieving a steady-state PV tracking efficiency exceeding 99\% and a buck converter power conversion efficiency of approximately 95\%. 
Furthermore, the system exhibits rapid transient recovery within milliseconds during sudden irradiance drops, ensuring stable and highly efficient energy transfer.

Simulation results confirm the effectiveness of the proposed method, achieving over 99\% steady-state PV tracking efficiency and around 95\% buck converter efficiency. The system exhibits rapid transient recovery within milliseconds under sudden irradiance changes, ensuring stable and efficient operation. The results demonstrate that the proposed hardware-efficient fuzzy MPPT achieves near-optimal performance with significantly reduced implementation complexity, making it highly suitable for fully integrated PV power management ICs.


\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
The integration of renewable energy sources, particularly photovoltaic (PV) systems, has become increasingly critical for sustainable power generation worldwide. However, the power output of PV arrays is inherently non-linear and heavily dependent on dynamic environmental conditions such as solar irradiance and temperature. 

To maximize energy extraction under these varying conditions, Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT) algorithms are employed. The primary benefit of an effective MPPT system in PV battery chargers is the assurance of optimal power transfer, which significantly reduces energy waste, accelerates charging times, and improves the overall system efficiency. 

In this work, we present an ASIC-oriented, three-input fuzzy logic MPPT controller, validated at the system level in MATLAB/Simulink. The proposed architecture achieves accurate and fast tracking by evaluating the change in PV voltage, change in output current, and absolute PV voltage. To pave the way for future low-cost integrated circuit design, we propose a hardware-friendly algorithmic mapping designed to avoid complex arithmetic operations. In the proposed hardware implementation, standard multipliers and dividers will be replaced with simple look-up tables and bitwise shift approximations. The overall system overview is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:system_overview}.

In the broader context of MPPT, conventional algorithms such as Perturb and Observe (P\&O) and Incremental Conductance (IC) are widely utilized \cite{po_ic}. The IC algorithm, for example, locates the MPP by evaluating the instantaneous and incremental conductance based on the slope of the power-voltage curve, effectively balancing tracking speed with steady-state accuracy. To address rapidly changing atmospheric conditions, these traditional methods are frequently augmented with variable step-sizes or intelligent control techniques, including neural networks and fuzzy logic \cite{fuzzy_review}. While this paper does not present a direct quantitative comparison against these baseline techniques, it builds upon the inherent strengths of fuzzy logic by introducing an algorithmic mapping specifically optimized to reduce computational complexity for future hardware integration.

The paper is organized as follows: Section \ref{sec:pv} describes the PV model, Section \ref{sec:architecture} details the system architecture, Section \ref{sec:results} presents the results, and Section \ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\subsection{Fuzzy Logic Controller}
\label{subsec:fuzzy_controller}

The fuzzy logic controller operates in three phases: Sampling \& Fuzzification, Inference, and Defuzzification \& Duty Adjustment.
The controller takes the three input parameters ($\Delta V_{pv}$, $\Delta I_{out}$, and $V_{pv}$) and produces the duty cycle change ($\Delta D$) as output.
The diagram is shown in Figure \ref{fig:fuzzy_diagram}.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{./images/matlab/fuzzy_controller_block_diagram.png}
\caption{Fuzzy logic controller block diagram showing the three phases of operation.}
\label{fig:fuzzy_diagram}
\end{figure*}

\subsubsection{Sampling \& Fuzzification}
In this phase, the three input parameters are sampled at the defined sampling frequency ($f_{sample} = 20$ kHz) using a 10-bit ADC for analog-to-digital converter. The first two parameters ($\Delta V_{pv}$ and $\Delta I_{out}$) are converted to digital form and their differences with previous sampled values are computed. The third parameter ($V_{pv}$) is taken directly without computing differences. These digital codes are then given to the inference part.

The degree of membership is determined by mapping the crisp digital input onto the corresponding linear segment of the membership function. The membership value, which ranges from 0 to 255, is extracted using linear interpolation between the defined vertices of the fuzzy set. To avoid complex division operations and maintain an ASIC-friendly architecture, it is proposed to approximate the interpolation fraction by rounding the denominator from 2046 to 2048 ($2^{11}$). For future hardware implementation, this enables the calculation to be simplified into a highly efficient bitwise right-shift ($\gg$) and addition:

\begin{equation}
    b = \frac{(a + 1023) \times 256}{2046} \approx (a \gg 3) + 128
    \label{eq:membership_calc}
\end{equation}
where $a$ represents the crisp digital input and $b$ represents the resulting 8-bit membership value.

\subsubsection{Inference}
The inference phase continuously evaluates a set of IF-THEN rules based on the three linguistic input variables: $\Delta V_{pv}$, $\Delta I_{out}$, and $V_{pv}$, using the \texttt{min} operator for the logical AND operation to determine the firing strength of each rule. In a standard three-input fuzzy system with three linguistic states each, a full combinatorial rule base would require 27 distinct rules ($3 \times 3 \times 3$). To significantly reduce computational complexity and silicon area, our proposed architecture streamlines this rule base. The absolute PV voltage ($V_{pv}$) acts as a primary safety and reference parameter. When $V_{pv}$ is operating far from the MPP voltage range, it overrides the other inputs to aggressively steer the system using only two boundary rules (Table \ref{tab:fuzzy_rules_safety}). When the voltage is within the optimal range, the controller evaluates only 9 rules based on the variations in voltage and current to precisely adjust the duty cycle, as defined in Table \ref{tab:fuzzy_rules_optimal}. This structural reduction from 27 to 11 total rules further optimizes the design for compact hardware implementation.

\subsubsection{Defuzzification \& Duty Adjustment}
In this final phase, a Sugeno-type weighted average is used to defuzzify the output of the inference process into a crisp value, $\Delta D$, according to the following equation:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta D = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i \cdot c_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i}
    \label{eq:defuzzification}
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the total number of active rules, $w_i$ represents the firing strength of the $i$-th rule obtained using the \texttt{min} operator, and $c_i$ is the constant crisp output coefficient defined for that rule. To enhance the ASIC-oriented architecture, hardware simplifications replace complex operations: the multiplication ($w_i \cdot c_i$) is approximated using a simple look-up table for conditional 1-bit right shifts, and the denominator summation (maximum 7 active rules) is converted into an efficient bitwise right-shift operation by 0, 1, 2, or 3 bits. The precision loss from these approximations is compensated by dividing the final output by a large factor, while a bumpless transfer mechanism ensures smooth transitions without abrupt adjustments to the converter's operation.

The performance of the proposed fuzzy logic-based MPPT controller was validated through a comprehensive simulation conducted in MATLAB/Simulink, with the overall results depicted in Figure \ref{fig:matlab_sim_overall}. The total simulation time was 0.6 seconds, designed to demonstrate the controller's performance during both steady-state and transient conditions. The converter efficiency, calculated as $P_{out}/P_{in}$, was maintained at approximately 95\% throughout the simulation. The PV efficiency, defined as $P_{in}/P_{vmax}$, is the primary metric for evaluating the MPPT algorithm's effectiveness.

The simulation is divided into three distinct phases, with the critical transient moments highlighted in detail in Figure \ref{fig:matlab_sim_zooms}(b) and (c).

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initial Stabilization (0s - 0.2s):} For the first 0.2 seconds, the system operates with a fixed duty cycle. The fuzzy logic controller is inactive, allowing the converter to stabilize without active MPPT control, as seen in the first section of Figure \ref{fig:matlab_sim_overall}.

    \item \textbf{Fuzzy Controller Activation (0.2s):} At 0.2 seconds, the fuzzy logic MPPT controller is enabled. Figure \ref{fig:sim_zoom_02} shows a zoomed-in view of this event. The controller rapidly adjusts the duty cycle to find the maximum power point, achieving over 99\% PV efficiency in just 13.4 milliseconds, which highlights its fast convergence speed.

    \item \textbf{Transient Response (0.4s):} At 0.4 seconds, a step change simulates a sudden decrease in solar irradiance. Figure \ref{fig:sim_zoom_04} details this transient. Despite the disturbance causing a momentary dip in PV efficiency to 90\%, the fuzzy controller responds swiftly, restoring the efficiency to over 99\% in approximately 1 millisecond.
\end{enumerate}

These results confirm the robustness and high performance of the fuzzy logic controller, showcasing its ability to quickly track the MPP and maintain high efficiency, even under rapidly changing conditions.

Table \ref{tab:steady_state_results} summarizes the steady-state performance of the proposed system under the evaluated varying irradiance conditions. The results demonstrate that the fuzzy logic controller maintains exceptional PV tracking efficiency while the buck converter sustains high power conversion efficiency across varying environmental states. Notably, the PV efficiency and buck converter efficiency remain around 99\% and 95\%, respectively, up until the converter enters Discontinuous Conduction Mode (DCM) at very low irradiance levels, which explains the efficiency drop observed at 200 $W/m^2$.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This paper presents an ASIC-oriented, three-input fuzzy logic MPPT controller for PV battery chargers that evaluates changes in PV voltage, output current, and absolute PV voltage to achieve highly responsive tracking. A major contribution is the proposed hardware-efficient architecture that replaces complex multipliers and dividers with simple look-up tables and bitwise shifts, significantly reducing implementation complexity for low-cost IC design. Simulation results demonstrate over 99\% PV tracking efficiency and validate the effectiveness of this approach compared to previous 2-input systems (Table \ref{tab:comparison}). The three-input architecture eliminates local minima trapping and maintains high steady-state stability at 500 kHz switching frequency, providing a favorable trade-off between tracking accuracy, transient response, and hardware complexity.

The proposed approach provides a favorable trade-off between tracking accuracy, transient response, and hardware complexity, highlighting its suitability for low-cost ASIC implementations. Future work will focus on the complete physical integrated circuit (IC) implementation and experimental hardware validation of the proposed system.
